01 · Problem first

Large networks are valuable only when they are retrievable.

LinkedIn supports people search through connection degree, location, company, school, industry, keywords, and conversational queries. Public feedback nevertheless shows that members can struggle when familiar title or keyword controls change, disappear, or produce results they cannot easily explain.

Problem statement
Active professionals with large first-degree networks routinely need to reach a specific person they already know — for a referral, a warm introduction, or domain advice — but can recall only that person's professional context, not their name. Today, finding that person means guessing keywords, scrolling connection lists manually, or paying for a prospecting tool built for cold outreach rather than personal recall. When the guess fails, members abandon the search or send a colder, less credible message to someone they don't actually know — so relationships already inside their network go unused exactly when they would matter most.

Standalone on purpose: no proposed solution appears above this line.

Official fact

People search has broad filters

LinkedIn documents connection degree, location, current company, past company, school, industry, and keyword filtering.

Official fact

Search is becoming conversational

LinkedIn states that updated people search can interpret meaning and accept role, title, company, and location in natural language.

Public signal

Users report lost precision

Recent community posts describe difficulty finding title filters and dissatisfaction with inconsistent or irrelevant AI-search results.

Product inference

Trust requires visibility

A hybrid experience should expose parsed filters, match reasons, and easy correction rather than replacing deterministic controls.

Observed workflow: a member knows the relationship and professional attributes of the person they need, but not the exact name. The current path can require trial-and-error across search text, filter menus, company pages, or premium products.
02 · PRD summary

Connection Lens

A lightweight decision document that states what to build, why now, how success will be measured, and what deliberately remains outside V1.

ProblemMembers cannot reliably retrieve relevant first-degree connections using remembered professional attributes.
SolutionA dedicated My Network search with explicit title, company, industry, location, relationship, and keyword chips.
Why nowConversational people search is expanding, while user feedback shows precision and explainability remain important.

Vision

Turn a member’s existing network into a useful professional memory system. A member should be able to describe who they need, verify the interpreted criteria, and act in under one minute.

Product principle

AI proposes. Filters prove. Natural language can accelerate query creation, but explicit chips, result explanations, and editable criteria preserve control.

03 · Persona and job

Build for purposeful network retrieval.

The persona is behavioral, not demographic, and the feature is intentionally separated from high-volume prospecting.

🔎

Primary persona

Active professionals with 500–5,000 first-degree connections who open LinkedIn at least 3–4 times a week and, roughly monthly, need to reach one specific person they already know — for a referral, an intro, or hiring/domain advice.

🎯

Jobs to Be Done

“When I need help with a specific professional goal, help me identify the most relevant person I already know so I can take a warm, credible next step.”

Core scenario

Find a first-degree connection at a target company whose current role is relevant to a job, partnership, or domain question.

Adjacent scenario

Rediscover former colleagues, alumni, or local contacts after a role change, relocation, or industry transition.

Not the target

Bulk lead generation, scraping, automated outreach, CRM enrichment, or recruiter-grade sourcing workflows.

04 · Success metrics — defined before the solution

Decide how success is measured before designing the fix.

One north star, two secondary metrics, and one guardrail — set immediately after the problem and persona, so every capability below is built to move a number, not just to ship a feature.

North Star

Successful Network Retrieval Rate (SNRR)

% of qualified search sessions where the member opens a relevant profile, messages, or saves a connection within ten minutes without re-querying out of frustration.

Secondary

Time to useful profile

Median elapsed time from search start to the first profile the member actually acts on.

Secondary

Parser correction rate

% of sessions where a member edits an auto-generated chip — a direct read on interpretation trust.

Guardrail

Unwanted-contact rate

Spam reports, blocks, and privacy complaints per 1,000 searches. Must not move upward as retrieval improves.

Working backward with an Opportunity Solution Tree

Each capability in this PRD exists because it traces back to the North Star outcome, not the other way around.

Outcome

Lift SNRR without harming trust or Premium.

→
Opportunities

Ambiguous titles, no match reasons, prospecting-tool overlap.

→
Solutions

Also monitored, not gated on: Sales Navigator and Recruiter cannibalization by cohort — a business signal to watch, kept separate from the single guardrail above so the guardrail stays unambiguous.

Happy path

Member enters a conversational query, confirms the generated chips, sees fewer than 25 relevant connections, and messages one.

Correction path

Member removes an incorrectly parsed title, broadens location, or changes current company to past company without rewriting the query.

Recovery path

Zero results triggers suggestions to relax one criterion at a time while preserving the original intent and privacy boundaries.

06 · Goals and non-goals

Scope is a product decision.

The V1 solves one job well: retrieving first-degree connections for member-led networking. Framed as a Now / Next / Later split, not a wishlist.

In scope for V1Out of scopeLater
First-degree connections only Second- and third-degree prospect discovery Connections-of and warm-introduction paths
Current title, current company, past company, industry, location, school, and keywords Revenue, headcount, intent signals, buyer personas, or CRM fields Skills, tenure, seniority, activity, and custom lists
Natural-language query translated into editable chips Autonomous outreach or message generation by default Optional message context assistant with approval
Result explanation and one saved search Exports, scraping, mass actions, or API access Multiple saved searches and alerts for Premium members
07 · Functional requirements

What V1 must do.

Requirements are ordered around query creation, trust, action, and safe recovery.

FR-01

Hybrid query builder

  • Accept natural language or manual filter selection.
  • Translate recognized entities into editable chips.
  • Preserve unrecognized text as keywords.
FR-02

Professional entity filters

  • Support current title, current company, past company, industry, location, school, and 1st-degree relationship.
  • Allow multi-select and include/exclude logic where safe.
FR-03

Explainable ranking

  • Show the criteria each result matched.
  • Rank exact current-role matches above weak keyword matches.
  • Never imply private information was used.
FR-04

Low-friction actions

  • Open profile, message, or save connection.
  • Show shared history already visible to the member.
  • Keep outreach member-initiated.
FR-05

Zero-result recovery

  • Identify the most restrictive criterion.
  • Recommend one reversible relaxation.
  • Show expected result-count change before applying.
FR-06

Saved intent

  • Permit one saved search in free V1.
  • Allow renaming and manual rerun.
  • No unsolicited alerts in V1.

MoSCoW protects the V1 decision.

The first release validates retrieval precision and member trust without recreating Sales Navigator.

10 · Product architecture

Natural language in. Auditable filters out.

The system performs permission-aware retrieval over attributes already visible to the searching member.

1. Query interfaceNatural language, manual filters, saved query
2. Entity parserTitle, company, location, school, industry, relationship intent
3. Member graph ACL1st-degree eligibility, visibility, blocks, privacy rules
4. Search indexNormalized professional entities and permitted profile fields
5. RankerExactness, currentness, relationship context, quality controls
6. Explanation layerMatched criteria, relaxations, feedback, telemetry

Request flow

query = parse(member_input) chips = normalize(query.entities) eligible_ids = connection_graph.first_degree(viewer_id) visible_ids = privacy_acl.filter(eligible_ids, viewer_id) candidates = search_index.retrieve( ids=visible_ids, filters=chips, keywords=query.remaining_text ) results = rank(candidates, exact_entity_match, current_role_match, relationship_context, profile_quality ) return explain(results, chips)

Reliability controls

• Deterministic fallback when parser confidence is low

• Title and company normalization with visible exact-versus-related semantics

• Blocked, hidden, and restricted members removed before retrieval

• Profile updates reflected through index freshness targets

• Query and explanation versioning for experiment analysis

• Abuse-rate limits and anomaly monitoring

13 · Edge cases

Resolve ambiguity before it becomes a trust failure.

The PRD exposes open questions rather than pretending they do not exist.

1

Ambiguous titles

“PM” may mean product manager, project manager, program manager, or preventive maintenance. Show interpreted titles and let members correct them.

2

Multiple current roles

Members can hold concurrent jobs. Match any current role, label which role matched, and avoid inferring primary employment.

3

Stale profiles

Display profile-reported data without asserting it is current beyond the member’s last update. Consider a freshness cue only when policy allows.

4

Very small networks

For fewer than 100 connections, default to a simplified search and do not overcomplicate the experience with advanced controls.

5

Sensitive inference

Do not support search over health, ethnicity, religion, political views, age, or other sensitive attributes, even when text appears on profiles.

6

Abuse and mass outreach

Rate-limit repetitive queries and messages, monitor spam signals, and omit bulk actions or exports.

14 · Open questions

What must be answered before launch.

These decisions require data, legal, trust, design, and monetization partners.

Product boundary

Which filters create enough personal-network utility without undermining Sales Navigator or Recruiter differentiation?

Ranking quality

Should recent interaction, shared history, or connection strength influence ranking, and how can that be explained safely?

Profile visibility

Which attributes are searchable when visible on a profile but hidden from a specific viewer through settings or policy?

Natural language

What parser confidence triggers automatic chips versus a clarification prompt or deterministic keyword fallback?

Saved searches

Does one free saved search increase repeat utility without creating a disguised prospecting workflow?

Messaging quality

Does improved retrieval lead to valued conversations, or simply increase unsolicited messages to high-demand members?

15 · Decision log

Make product judgment visible.

A decision log shows why the PRD chooses restraint over feature accumulation.

First-degree onlySolves personal network retrieval, reduces abuse, and protects premium prospecting products.
Explicit chipsPreserves transparency when natural-language interpretation is wrong or incomplete.
No bulk actionsKeeps the workflow member-led and reduces spam, scraping, and automation incentives.
One free saved searchTests repeat utility without immediately turning the feature into an alerting or lead-management system.
No message generator in V1Separates retrieval quality from outreach quality and avoids masking whether the right person was found.
16 · Tradeoffs

A tight scope is a bet, not a free lunch.

Every restraint decision above has a cost. Naming it here is what separates a defensible scope call from wishful thinking.

Solve one job perfectly

Gain

A narrow V1 that fully solves one use case — first-degree retrieval — ships faster and lets us learn faster from real usage.

Cost

Underserves adjacent users. Scoping this tightly means recruiters, second-degree prospectors, and bulk networkers get a half-solution until V2.

Clear success criteria

Gain

Defining SNRR, the two secondary metrics, and the guardrail before build prevents scope creep and post-launch arguments about what "success" means.

Cost

Rigidity risk. If discovery in Weeks 1–2 surfaces a different job to solve, a metrics-first PRD can become a constraint that's harder to unwind mid-build.

17 · Delivery plan

Six weeks to a decision-quality test.

The plan aligns Search, My Network, Trust, Premium, Data Science, Design, and Messaging partners.

Week 1

Discovery and baseline

Analyze My Network search funnels, zero-result queries, title corrections, premium overlap, spam risk, and member interviews.

Week 2

Prototype and policy

Validate filter comprehension, chip correction, result explanations, zero-state recovery, privacy constraints, and monetization boundaries.

Weeks 3–4

Build and instrument

Implement entry point, parser integration, ACL filtering, index query, ranking, explanation payload, events, and feature flag.

Week 5

Dogfood and ramp

Validate relevance, latency, parser failures, privacy exclusions, abuse limits, and premium guardrail dashboards at 1% exposure.

Week 6+

A/B test and decide

Read SNRR, time to useful profile, corrections, message quality, trust signals, segment effects, and premium impact.

18 · Interview narrative

The 90-second executive readout.

Present the out-of-scope decision before the feature details.

“The scope deliberately avoids building a free version of Sales Navigator. It focuses on one member job: retrieving a relevant person from an existing first-degree network. LinkedIn’s current help documentation shows that people search supports filters such as connection degree, company, location, industry, school, and keywords, and that search is becoming more conversational. Recent public feedback also shows that members can lose trust when title controls change or AI results feel imprecise. Connection Lens combines natural-language input with explicit editable chips. The member can search for ‘product leaders at healthcare companies in Chicago,’ verify how the system interpreted title, industry, and location, and see why each connection matched. V1 is limited to first-degree connections, has no exports, no automated outreach, and only one saved search. Measurement centers Successful Network Retrieval Rate, time to useful profile, parser corrections, result relevance, message quality, privacy complaints, and premium cannibalization. The core product judgment is that AI can accelerate query construction, but deterministic filters and explanations must preserve member control.”
