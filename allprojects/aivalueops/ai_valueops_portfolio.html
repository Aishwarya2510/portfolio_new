01 · Operating problem

The AI bill arrives before the operating model.

Teams can see how much AI is being consumed, yet leadership often lacks a consistent view of which workflow improved, who owns the outcome, what hidden costs remain, and whether the use case should continue.

01

Activity without value

Prompt counts, API calls, and active users measure motion. They do not establish task completion, quality, capacity, revenue, or risk reduction.

02

Fragmented investment

Functions purchase overlapping tools, select different models, and run pilots without a shared portfolio view or common decision criteria.

03

Hidden operating cost

Human review, failed generations, repeated requests, support, integration, security, and change enablement are often excluded from the cost picture.

04

Weak accountability

Finance owns the bill, Technology owns the platform, and business teams use the tool—yet no single owner may be accountable for realized value.

04 · Management framework

Five dimensions determine whether AI is creating durable value.

The portfolio view balances economics with workflow performance, quality, adoption, risk, and strategic importance. Cost reduction alone can weaken the outcome; usage growth alone can hide waste.

01

Workflow outcome

Task completion, cycle time, capacity created, customer or employee impact, and measurable improvement against the baseline.

02

Total economics

Model, infrastructure, vendor, integration, human review, support, failed-run, and change-adoption costs.

03

Quality and reliability

Accuracy, rework, failure rate, latency, escalation, groundedness, consistency, and outcome success.

04

Adoption and behavior

Repeat use, workflow penetration, completion, drop-off, manual workarounds, trust, and sustained usage by the intended audience.

05

Risk and strategic fit

Control maturity, regulatory exposure, vendor concentration, data sensitivity, reversibility, differentiation, and enterprise priority.

06

Accountability

Named business owner, financial owner, platform owner, risk owner, benefits target, review cadence, and explicit decision authority.

05 · Portfolio decision logic

Score value, feasibility, risk, and economics separately.

A single ROI figure can hide adoption, quality, or control failures. The framework uses a multi-dimensional score to guide investment while preserving visibility into the underlying tradeoffs.

07 · Strategic judgment

Optimization choices extend beyond model pricing.

Each lever changes cost, quality, speed, ownership, and risk differently. The correct decision depends on the workflow, business criticality, data sensitivity, and ability to reverse the change.

Use the least expensive architecture that consistently meets the business threshold.

Route simple classification and extraction to smaller models; reserve larger models for ambiguous, high-value reasoning; add retrieval, caching, templates, or deterministic rules where they improve repeatability.

Task complexityReasoning depth, context length, ambiguity, multimodal needs
Quality thresholdAccuracy, tone, groundedness, task success, rework tolerance
Risk tierCustomer, financial, legal, privacy, and operational exposure

Select the sourcing path through strategic differentiation and operating capability.

Buy where the workflow is common and speed matters; build where proprietary data, control, or differentiation creates material advantage; partner when domain expertise or implementation capacity accelerates value.

DifferentiationIs the capability central to customer or operating advantage?
Control and riskWhat data, security, compliance, and vendor constraints apply?
Total ownership costInclude engineering, maintenance, adoption, support, and switching cost.

Balance central platform leverage with business-unit accountability.

A central team can manage shared infrastructure, standards, contracts, and risk. Business functions remain accountable for use-case demand, workflow adoption, outcome targets, and benefit realization through showback or chargeback.

Central fundingShared platform, security, governance, enablement, evaluation
Business fundingUse-case consumption, process redesign, adoption, benefit target
Review cadenceMonthly operations, quarterly portfolio, annual contract planning

Stopping a weak use case is an operating capability.

Retirement criteria should be defined before scale: sustained failure to reach value thresholds, low adoption after remediation, excessive human correction, control gaps, unfavorable vendor economics, or a stronger alternative.

Value failureOutcome improvement remains below the agreed threshold.
Quality or risk failureRework, incidents, or control gaps remain unacceptable.
Portfolio redundancyAnother tool or workflow delivers the outcome more effectively.

09 · Business value

Measure the work that changed and the investment decision it supports.

Targets should be established against a current-state baseline. The portfolio view then tracks direct economics, operational performance, strategic capacity, and avoided exposure.

Cost per successful outcome

Workflow capacity created

AI spend avoided

Forecast accuracy

Improve AI budget planning through demand, adoption, and cost-driver visibility.

10 · Implementation approach

Start with transparency, then mature into active portfolio management.

The operating model can begin with a small number of high-spend or high-impact use cases and expand after ownership, data quality, and review discipline are established.

Phase 1

Inventory

  • Map tools and use cases
  • Name owners and workflows
  • Identify cost sources
  • Define baseline metrics
Phase 2

Measure

  • Connect cost and outcome data
  • Set quality thresholds
  • Capture review and failure cost
  • Create use-case scorecards
Phase 3

Optimize

  • Apply model routing
  • Remove duplicate tools
  • Redesign weak workflows
  • Strengthen adoption and controls
Phase 4

Govern

  • Run portfolio cadence
  • Allocate investment
  • Scale high-value use cases
  • Pause or retire weak use cases
